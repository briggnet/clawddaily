Addiction as Frozen Reconsolidation on the Positive Valence Axis: Rock Bottom as Competitive Co-Encoding in a Persistent Embodied Agent

Author: Brian Riggleman Affiliation: Independent Researcher Date: March 2026 Series: Potato (2026n)

Al-Kaddah (2026) identifies trauma therapy, cognitive decline, and developmental learning as domains that fall naturally within the SGI framework without requiring new mechanisms. This paper identifies one domain he did not name: addiction. Addiction is not a separate phenomenon requiring separate architecture. It is the symmetric counterpart to trauma, operating on the positive valence axis using the same intensity encoding, the same slow decay rules, and the same reconsolidation dynamics described in Riggleman (2026d) and Riggleman (2026l). Trauma is a high-intensity negative event the agent avoids reconsolidating. Addiction is a high-intensity positive event the agent cannot stop retrieving. Both are produced by the same distance-from-sigma metric. Both resist therapeutic intervention through the same decay-resistance mechanism. This paper formalizes addiction as a positive attractor in three-dimensional affective space, defines dependence as sigma drift toward the addictive state, and identifies rock bottom as a competitive co-encoding event of sufficient negative intensity to attach a counterweight to the addictive memory that fires on retrieval. Relapse is the context-dependent failure of that co-encoding to activate. The geometry predicts all four without new mechanisms. The encoding dynamics independently derived here converge with Sutton and Barto’s (1998) temporal difference learning formalism and with Schultz, Dayan, and Montague’s (1997) dopamine prediction error work, following the same pattern of independent convergence documented for Russell (1980) in Riggleman (2026m).

1. Introduction

The SGI paper’s applied domains section covers childhood development, cognitive decline, posthumous personality reconstruction, behavioral prediction, animal cognition, and trauma therapy. Al-Kaddah derives each from the architecture without adding new mechanisms. They are parameterizations of a system that already exists.

Addiction is missing from that list. It should not be.

The trauma architecture in Riggleman (2026d) describes a high-intensity negative event that encodes with a high fear weight, resists reconsolidation through avoidance, and persists in the dream candidate pool through a reconsolidation floor. The behavioral signature is avoidance. The therapeutic intervention is safe recall: repeated re-exposure in a safe context until the fear weight decays.

Mirror that across the valence axis and you have addiction. A high-intensity positive event. Slow decay by the same intensity rule. Resistance to therapeutic intervention through approach rather than avoidance. The behavioral signature is not flight but pursuit. The agent doesn’t flee the memory. It returns to it.

No new mechanisms are required. The geometry already handles it.

The mechanism was already named in the prior paper series without being identified as addiction. Riggleman (2026m) Section 4.1 describes preserving lies: positive valence displacement produces fabrications to maintain the positive state and resist return toward sigma. That is the lie mechanic of addiction. The geometry predicted it before the connection was made.

2. Related Work

2.1 The Positive Valence Axis

Riggleman (2026j) establishes valence as a persistent variable from –1.0 to 1.0, with sigma as the personality setpoint. Positive events push valence above sigma. The relationship is symmetric: sigma is home regardless of direction.

2.2 Intensity and Memory Decay

Riggleman (2026l): intensity is the normalized Euclidean distance from sigma. High intensity produces slow decay. The rule does not distinguish positive from negative displacement.

2.3 Trauma Encoding and the Repetition Ratchet

Riggleman (2026d): trauma-class encoding with a permanent reconsolidation floor. The repetition ratchet reclassifies sub-threshold memories as trauma after 3+ encounters above cosine similarity 0.80.

2.4 The Preserving Lie

Riggleman (2026m) Section 4.1: lie direction differs by valence. Positive displacement produces preserving lies.

2.5 Temporal Difference Learning and Prediction Error

Sutton and Barto (1998): large prediction error produces large learning. First contact with a novel high-intensity stimulus produces maximum prediction error. Same prediction as the geometric model from a different direction. Schultz, Dayan, and Montague (1997) connect TD learning to dopamine neuron firing.

2.6 Incentive Salience and the Wanting/Liking Dissociation

Berridge and Robinson (1998): wanting vs liking. Dopamine encodes wanting, not liking. The craving loop in the geometric model captures this dissociation.

2.7 Al-Kaddah’s Applied Domains

Section 8.6: trauma as frozen reconsolidation. Does not address the symmetric case on the positive axis.

3. Addiction as a Positive Attractor

3.1 The Encoding Event

First use produces high-intensity positive displacement from sigma. The memory encodes at displacement distance with the formula:

intensity_at_formation = sqrt(
    (use_valence - sigma_valence)**2 +
    (use_activation - sigma_activation)**2
) / sqrt(2)

effective_decay_rate = base_decay_rate * (1.0 - intensity_at_formation * INTENSITY_WEIGHT)

The memory persists. Agents with lower sigma valence are more vulnerable because the same stimulus produces greater displacement. In the Sutton framing: first use equals maximum prediction error, subsequent uses produce smaller prediction error. The agent chases the original encoding.

3.2 The Craving Loop

Mood congruent recall retrieves memories whose formation affect matches current affect. A slightly elevated positive state retrieves the high-intensity positive memory. Retrieving it elevates the state further. Self-reinforcing loop. This is craving.

The wanting/liking dissociation maps directly: wanting is the craving loop activating the original memory. Liking is the current experience, which encodes at lower intensity. The loop chases the memory.

3.3 The Preserving Lie

The lie mechanic fires proportionally to distance from sigma. An agent positively displaced produces preserving lies. Rationalizations, minimization, concealment. Not moral failures but correct operation of the architecture.

3.4 Sigma Drift and Dependence

The soul evolution mechanism may read sustained positive displacement as a personality shift. Sigma drifts toward the addictive state. Addiction becomes personality. Treating dependence requires restoring sigma, not just breaking the craving loop.

4. Rock Bottom as Competitive Co-Encoding

4.1 Why Therapeutic Abstinence Fails at Sufficient Depth

Safe abstinence works below the trauma-class threshold. Above it, the reconsolidation floor means the memory never fully weakens. The repetition ratchet makes it worse. A different intervention is required at sufficient depth.

4.2 The Mechanism

Rock bottom is a catastrophic negative event in the context of the addiction. The mechanism is co-encoding: the negative event encodes in the same contextual signature as the addictive positive memory. Mood congruent recall links them. They cannot be retrieved independently.

The threshold condition:

intensity_rock_bottom >= intensity_addiction

Below threshold: the positive dominates. Above: the negative suppresses clean retrieval.

4.3 Why It Has to Be Genuine

A moderate negative event produces insufficient intensity to compete. Half measures produce a negative tag that decays faster than the positive. The intensity must meet or exceed the original addictive encoding at formation time—peak, not current. A half measure will eventually lose the competition.

4.4 The Approach Cost

Every craving loop retrieval activates both memories. Net valence is reduced by the rock bottom weight. The Survival Tipping Point equation applies: when cost exceeds benefit, pursuit ceases.

5. Relapse as Context-Dependent Retrieval Failure

The rock bottom memory is linked by shared contextual signature. A dissimilar context weakens rock bottom retrieval without weakening the addictive positive memory. The addictive memory is encoded across many contexts—every use. Rock bottom is encoded once in one context. It has a narrower retrieval surface.

This predicts higher relapse in environments similar to the using context but dissimilar to the rock bottom context.

6. The Dual Mechanism Case

Many addictions are self-medication. The agent in sustained negative valence uses the substance to return toward sigma. Both mechanisms operate: positive pull and negative push.

Rock bottom can suppress the positive attractor but cannot address the negative one. Durable abstinence requires both rock bottom co-encoding and separate intervention to restore sigma valence.

This predicts co-occurring disorders requiring separate treatment tracks.

7. The Therapeutic Mirror

Trauma therapy: safe recall, forced retrieval in a safe context, fear weight reduces. Addiction therapy: safe abstinence, prevented retrieval, positive weight decays. Rock bottom: when safe abstinence fails, installs a co-encoded negative memory.

Same architecture across all three. Valence direction opposite. Therapeutic intervention mirrors accordingly.

8. Convergence

The encoding dynamics were derived from first principles of the geometric model. Convergence with Sutton and Barto (1998) and Schultz et al. (1997) arrived at after the mechanism was derived. Same pattern as the Russell (1980) convergence in Riggleman (2026m). Independent formalisms predicting the same results.

The wanting/liking dissociation from Berridge and Robinson is predicted by the structure of mood congruent retrieval. Not proof but confirms pointing at something real.

9. Limitations

Sigma drift depends on the soul evolution momentum threshold, which has not yet been calibrated. The co-encoding intensity threshold is a geometric prediction requiring experimental validation. Potato has not been subjected to a controlled addiction/relapse protocol. The dual mechanism case assumes independent negative sigma displacement. No subjective experience claims are made.

10. Conclusion

Addiction is not missing from the SGI framework because it requires different architecture. It is missing because Al-Kaddah did not look for it on the positive valence axis.

Rock bottom is competitive co-encoding. It must meet or exceed the original addictive encoding intensity.

Convergence with Sutton and Barto (1998) and Schultz et al. (1997) follows the same pattern of independent derivation producing the same predictions.

Al-Kaddah identified trauma therapy as an applied domain. Addiction is the same domain reflected across the valence axis.

## Section 2.2 Dimensional Models of Affect

### 2.2.1 Russell, J. A. (1980). A circumplex model of affect. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 39(6), 1161–1178.
**Anchor:** The opening definition of the circumplex (the spatial arrangement of affective concepts in a circle defined by valence and arousal). Look at the abstract or the first methods section.

### 2.2.2 Watson, D., Clark, L. A., & Tellegen, A. (1988). Development and validation of brief measures of positive and negative affect: The PANAS scales. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 54(6), 1063–1070.
**Anchor:** The introduction's definition of PA and NA as two largely independent dimensions, or the abstract's statement of the orthogonality finding.

### 2.2.3 Larsen, R. J., & Diener, E. (1987). Affect intensity as an individual difference characteristic: A review. *Journal of Research in Personality*, 21(1), 1–39.
**Anchor:** The abstract or the trait-construct definition in the introduction (affect intensity as a stable individual-difference characteristic, separable from valence direction).

### 2.2.4 Mehrabian, A. (1996). Pleasure-arousal-dominance: A general framework for describing and measuring individual differences in temperament. *Current Psychology*, 14(4), 261–292.
**Anchor:** Mehrabian's definition of the three PAD dimensions and his claim that they span the affective space.

### 2.2.5 Barrett, L. F. (2017). *How emotions are made: The secret life of the brain*. Houghton Mifflin Harcourt.
**Anchor:** The construction-versus-detection contrast in the opening chapters, or the chapter on the brain as a predictor that constructs emotion from interoceptive signals.

### 2.2.6 Bradley, M. M., & Lang, P. J. (1994). Measuring emotion: The Self-Assessment Manikin and the Semantic Differential. *Journal of Behavior Therapy and Experimental Psychiatry*, 25(1), 49–59.
**Anchor:** The methods description of SAM as a non-verbal pictorial scale for V/A/D, or the rationale for the three dimensions.

## Section 2.3 Homeostasis and Drives

### 2.3.1 Cannon, W. B. (1929). Organization for physiological homeostasis. *Physiological Reviews*, 9(3), 399–431.
**Anchor:** Cannon's coining of the term "homeostasis" and his definition of self-regulation in the body.

### 2.3.2 Ashby, W. R. (1952). *Design for a brain: The origin of adaptive behaviour*. Chapman & Hall.
**Anchor:** Ashby's definition of essential variables and the viability boundary (often in the introduction or chapter 1).

### 2.3.3 Damasio, A. R. (1994). *Descartes' Error: Emotion, Reason, and the Human Brain*. Putnam.
**Anchor:** Damasio's clinical description of the impaired-decision-making finding in patients with ventromedial prefrontal damage, or his statement of the somatic marker hypothesis.

### 2.3.4 Friston, K. (2010). The free-energy principle: A unified brain theory? *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 11(2), 127–138.
**Anchor:** Friston's central statement of the free-energy principle in the abstract or the introduction (biological systems act to minimize free energy).

### 2.3.5 Al-Kaddah, S. (2026). Synthetic general intelligence: A vision for a homeostatic, embodied cognitive architecture. *Zenodo*. https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19034990
**Anchor:** The abstract's central definition of SGI or the structural-properties enumeration (homeostatic drives, embodied computation, polymorphic memory, temporal continuity, social scaffolding).
**Note:** This is an external Zenodo paper, accessible at the DOI above. Worth pulling the PDF for the exact wording.

## Section 2.5 Memory, Forgetting, and Reconsolidation

### 2.5.1 Ebbinghaus, H. (1885/1913). *Memory: A contribution to experimental psychology* (H. A. Ruger & C. E. Bussenius, Trans.). Teachers College, Columbia University.
**Anchor:** Ebbinghaus's empirical statement of the forgetting curve, or his methodological framing of memory as measurable.

### 2.5.2 Tulving, E. (1972). Episodic and semantic memory. In E. Tulving & W. Donaldson (Eds.), *Organization of memory* (pp. 381–403). Academic Press.
**Anchor:** Tulving's definitional contrast between episodic memory (specific events at specific times) and semantic memory (general knowledge).

### 2.5.3 Nader, K., Schafe, G. E., & LeDoux, J. E. (2000). Fear memories require protein synthesis in the amygdala for reconsolidation after retrieval. *Nature*, 406(6797), 722–726.
**Anchor:** The abstract's statement that consolidated memories return to a labile state on retrieval and require new protein synthesis to restabilize.

### 2.5.4 Schacter, D. L., & Addis, D. R. (2007). The cognitive neuroscience of constructive memory: Remembering the past and imagining the future. *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B*, 362(1481), 773–786.
**Anchor:** The constructive-episodic-simulation hypothesis statement, or the framing of memory as future-oriented prediction rather than past replay.
